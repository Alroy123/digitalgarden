# The First Blood

More than seven hundred years before the mountain city of Antilla ever rose from stone, before the noble houses carved their banners into the halls of power, there lived four siblings whose names would one day be whispered in fear and reverence alike.

The eldest was Marsen, a tall and quiet young man whose shoulders carried the weight of responsibility long before he had grown into them. Even as a boy he had stood between danger and his family without hesitation. His strength was not loud, nor boastful, but steady as iron buried deep in the earth.

Second was Viola, a woman whose beauty was spoken of wherever travelers gathered. Yet it was not merely beauty that drew people to her. There was a calm about her, a quiet authority that settled over a room the moment she entered it. Her voice could soothe anger, calm fear, and bend even the most stubborn hearts toward her will.

Third came Silas.

Silas burned with life where Marsen endured and Viola guided. He was passionate, ambitious, restless. He refused to believe that the world must always remain cruel and unforgiving. Where others saw misery as fate, Silas saw it as something to be conquered.

And last was Margaret—Maggie to those who loved her.

She was the soul of the family, the warmth in the cold years, the laughter that softened even Marsen’s hardened expressions. In a life filled with hunger and fear, Maggie’s kindness was the one thing that never faltered.

Their lives were brutal beyond imagining. In those days the land was plagued by terror, ruled by a tyrant known as Guildaxe, the Lord of Nightmares. From the Shadowfell he sent forth an endless tide of monsters—wraiths, shadow beasts, horrors that crept through darkness like living nightmares.

Wherever villages dared to grow, Guildaxe’s armies descended.

And so the siblings’ family wandered endlessly, never daring to settle for long. Days passed when their stomachs held nothing but water and bitter roots. Weeks came where sleep meant curling against one another in the cold, praying the night would pass quietly.

For years they survived this way, always hiding, always running, always wondering if the next sunrise would be their last.

Until the night it was.

Guildaxe’s army came like a storm without warning. Shadows poured down upon their village as screams split the air. The siblings Could do nothing but watch as everything was destroyed, for what can mortals do against creatures born of darkness. By morning the village was gone. Their parents lay dead among the ruins, everyone they had known was slaughtered.

Only the four siblings remained.

They fled without direction, without hope, driven only by the need to escape. They ran until their shoes split apart on jagged stones, until their feet bled and their lungs burned like fire in their chests. Still they ran, afraid that if they slowed even for a moment the shadows would return to finish what they had begun. 

And there, upon the cold stone slopes, the siblings collapsed. Death was coming quietly now. Marsen could barely breathe, Maggie’s lips had turned blue with cold. Viola lay staring at the dark sky, too weak even to cry. Silas was the only one who noticed it. A faint light flickering in the darkness. 
The hut was small, its roof made of worn thatch and its chimney leaning at a crooked angle. Hanging beside the door was a broken wooden sign swaying gently from a single chain.

“Follow me,” he rasped to his siblings.

One by one, they crawled after him.

Inside waited a woman who seemed both impossibly ancient. Her skin as purple as an amethyst, and her eyes shone with an otherworldly light that seemed older than the mountains themselves. She wore robes of deep red and black velvet that caught the firelight like flowing wine. She was an Archfey—one of the ancient beings whose power shaped the hidden corners of the world. 
For weeks she cared for the siblings. She fed them, tended their wounds, and let them rest beside the warmth of her hearth while winter winds screamed across the mountains outside. 

Slowly their strength returned.

One night, when the others slept, Silas sat across from her by the fire.

“I never want to feel loss again,” he said quietly. “And I never want my family to suffer loss like we did.”
